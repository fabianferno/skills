# Hackathon problem-first ideation

## Stance

**Starting from track prizes and working backward** usually produces a **forced** concept: the story, demo, and “why this exists” do not hang together for judges or real users.

Prefer the opposite order: **problem and product truth first**, **prize fit second**.

---

## Recommended order

1. **Spot the anchor** — Real-world pain, friction, or a **credible trend** (tech, regulation, behavior, market) you can explain in one sentence without mentioning a sponsor.
2. **Define the product** — A **narrow MVP** that would still be useful if no hackathon existed: clear user, one core loop, obvious value.
3. **Raise UX and relatability** — **Good usability**, clear flows, copy that non-experts understand. Judges should **recognize the scenario** (work, money, health, climate, creativity, etc.) without crypto or API trivia.
4. **Map prizes and tracks last** — After the product story is solid, **honestly** list which tracks or sponsor bounties it **naturally** touches. Stretch one **primary** alignment; treat secondary fits as optional talking points, not the spine of the pitch.

If a prize cannot align without rewriting the whole idea, **keep the product** and accept that track as non-target—or adjust scope only where the change **still** improves the real-world story.

---

## Why this wins hackathons

- Demos read as **products**, not “API homework.”
- Q&A stays on **user value** instead of contrived sponsor narratives.
- Prize alignment feels **earned**, which judges and sponsors tend to reward more than checkbox integrations.

---

## Anti-patterns

- Choosing the largest bounty first and inventing a problem to match.
- Burying the user story under **three sponsor logos** and no clear job-to-be-done.
- Claiming five tracks with equal enthusiasm when the build only proves one.
